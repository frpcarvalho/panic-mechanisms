POSSIBLE EXPLANATIONS
----------------------

1. SAMPLING BIAS:
   - Wealthier individuals may have better access to healthcare
   - More likely to receive PD diagnosis vs misdiagnosis
   - NHANES may undersample severely impoverished with undiagnosed PD
   → CANNOT RULE OUT

2. URBAN vs RURAL EFFECT:
   - Poverty-income ratio may be proxy for urban living
   - Urban environments: more stressors, faster pace, social pressure
   - PD more prevalent in urban settings (known in literature)
   → PLAUSIBLE MECHANISM

3. SOCIOECONOMIC STRESS HYPOTHESIS:
   - "Keeping up with the Joneses" anxiety
   - Performance pressure in professional environments
   - Fear of status loss
   - Work-life balance issues in demanding careers
   → BIOLOGICALLY PLAUSIBLE

4. LIFESTYLE FACTORS:
   - Sedentary white-collar work
   - High caffeine consumption (professional culture)
   - Sleep deprivation
   - Social isolation despite wealth
   → BEHAVIORAL MEDIATORS

5. HEALTH AWARENESS BIAS:
   - Wealthier individuals more aware of symptoms
   - More likely to seek mental health treatment
   - Less stigma in higher socioeconomic groups
   → DETECTION BIAS

6. REVERSE CAUSATION:
   - PD onset may lead to career advancement disruption
   - But PIR measured at same time as diagnosis
   → UNLIKELY but cannot fully exclude

CONCLUSION
----------
The "Poverty Paradox" in NHANES data is a robust finding that likely
reflects a complex interplay of:
1. Healthcare access and diagnostic bias
2. Urban living as mediator
3. Socioeconomic stress in high-achieving populations
4. Lifestyle factors associated with wealth

This finding does NOT mean poverty is protective - rather, it suggests
that the relationship between socioeconomic status and PD is more
nuanced than simple linear association.

Further research with longitudinal design and urban/rural stratification
is needed to disentangle these mechanisms.
